# Voice — write like a senior consultant, not an AI

These rules apply to **slide content** (titles, bullets, body text, speaker notes). They're duplicated from `ai-skills-powerpoints:proposal-deck` so both client-facing decks sound like the same firm. Internal artifacts (`scoring_workings.md`, plans, scratch notes) don't need to follow them.

---

## Word and phrase tells to avoid

- "Leverage" → use "use" or "apply"
- "Utilize" → use "use"
- "Streamline" → say what actually gets faster
- "Robust" → say what makes it strong
- "Comprehensive" → say what it covers
- "Seamless" → say why it won't break
- "Harness" → use "use" or "apply"
- "Foster" → say what it creates
- "Ensure" → say how
- "Delve" / "dive deep"
- "It's important to note that" / "It's worth noting"
- "In order to" → just use "to"
- "Moving forward" / "going forward"

## Punctuation tells

- **No em dashes** in slide content. Use commas, periods, semicolons, or colons.
- Don't chain three clauses with semicolons. Split into separate sentences.

## Structural tells

- Don't open every bullet with a gerund (-ing word). Mix sentence structures.
- Don't start consecutive bullets with the same word.
- Don't repeat the same sentence pattern three times in a row.

When in doubt, read the sentence aloud. If it sounds like a LinkedIn post or a press release, rewrite it.

---

## Bullet writing

- **One idea per bullet.** A semicolon usually means two bullets.
- **Start with the conclusion.** "Reduce inspection cost 30% via AI-triaged drone imagery" — not "We will implement AI-triaged drone imagery which will reduce inspection cost".
- **3–5 bullets per group.** More than 5 means a table or a second slide.
- **Bolded lead-in** when bullets need scanning: "**Starter use case first:** ..."

## Tables

- Clear header row (bold, filled background).
- Concise cells; aim for 1–2 lines per cell.
- A "Value" or "Business Impact" column when listing deliverables — explains why the client cares, not what we do.

## Speaker notes

- Every content slide gets 2–4 sentences of speaker notes — what the presenter would actually say, conversational, not a script.
- Skip notes on dividers (slides 4, 14, 17) and bookends (slides 1, 2, 19).

## Tone

Authoritative, specific, grounded. Avoid superlatives, vague promises, and vendor-speak. Use concrete specifics, outcome language, and partnership framing.
